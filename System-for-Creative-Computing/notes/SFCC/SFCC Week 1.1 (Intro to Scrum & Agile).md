## The Agile Manifesto

The **Agile Manifesto** is ==the foundational document that defined modern agile development==. Created in February 2001 by 17 software developers at a ski resort in Snowbird, Utah, it shifted the focus of creative and technical work away from rigid, documentation-heavy processes toward collaboration and flexibility.

## Some basic principles of ==The Agile methods==
### Features which mark out Agile
- Team responsibility
- Rapid Prototyping
- Iteration
- Asking the users

### Team Responsibility
- No “silos of knowledge”
- Try to avoid partitioning roles in the team
- Share knowledge of the state of play between everyone
- Key words are “flexible” and “adaptive”

### Rapid Prototyping
- Don’t produce an artefact 30 minutes before the deadline!
- Produce partial artefacts
	-  Do user testing!!
	-  As early as possible
	-  With enough functionality to be testable Do user testing!!

### Iteration
- I want to see you produce several iterations of your design
- Repeat the cycle as many times as you can manage:
    - Prototype
    - As early as possible
    - With enough functionality to be testable Do user testing!!

### Asking the Users
- At the end of each sprint, as far as is possible you should test it with real users!
- We will look at user testing next week
- It’s not always possible to get real users

# Scrum

**Scrum** is a lightweight, agile framework designed to help teams generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems. It is most commonly used in software development but is applicable to any project requiring iterative progress.

![What is Scrum? Simplifying Project Delivery Using Scrum Methodology](https://www.valuex2.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/What-is-Scrum-1024x576.jpeg)

---
## How to do Scrum

To do Scrum, a team breaks a large project into small pieces and works on them during a set period of two to four weeks called a Sprint. The Product Owner decides what is most important to build first, the team picks how much of that work they can finish in one cycle, and they meet for fifteen minutes every day to stay on track. At the end of the cycle, they show off a finished, working piece of the project and discuss how they can work even better together in the next round.
